\title{%
  A Rigorous Proof Framework for Lower Bounds on the\\
  Autoconvolution Constant $C_{1a}$\\[6pt]
  \large Via the Cloninger--Steinerberger Branch-and-Prune Algorithm
}
\author{Jai Bajaj}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We present a self-contained proof that the autoconvolution constant
$C_{1a}$ --- the infimum of
$\|f * f\|_{L^\infty} / \bigl(\int f\bigr)^2$ over nonnegative
functions supported on $[-\tfrac14, \tfrac14]$ --- satisfies a lower
bound certifiable by exhaustive computation.  Our starting point is the
framework of Cloninger and Steinerberger (2017), which we extend with a
multiscale cascade and tighter pruning.  The paper is organized around
the natural sequence of reductions: from continuous functions to bin
averages (Section~\ref{sec:bin}), from the continuous simplex to a
finite lattice (Section~\ref{sec:disc}), and finally to an exhaustive
search made tractable by pruning
(Sections~\ref{sec:testvalue}--\ref{sec:cascade}).
Section~\ref{sec:correctness} addresses the invariants that keep the
computation honest.  Throughout, we emphasize intuition alongside formal
statements.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{The Constant $C_{1a}$}

Consider the class of nonnegative, integrable functions supported on
the interval $[-\tfrac14, \tfrac14]$:
\[
  \mathcal{F} \;=\; \Bigl\{
    f : \R \to \R_{\ge 0}
    \;\Big|\;
    \supp(f) \subseteq \bigl[-\tfrac14, \tfrac14\bigr],\;
    \int f > 0
  \Bigr\}.
\]

The \emph{autoconvolution} of $f$ is
\[
  (f * f)(t) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\, f(t - x)\, dx.
\]
Since $\supp(f) \subseteq [-\tfrac14, \tfrac14]$, the
autoconvolution is supported on $[-\tfrac12, \tfrac12]$ and is itself
nonnegative.

A natural question is: how small can the peak of $f * f$ be, relative
to the total mass of $f$?  This is precisely what $C_{1a}$ measures.

\begin{definition}
The \emph{autoconvolution constant} is
\[
  C_{1a} \;=\; \inf_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \;
  \frac{\displaystyle\max_{|t| \le 1/2}\, (f*f)(t)}%
       {\displaystyle\left(\int f\right)^{\!2}}.
\]
\end{definition}

By homogeneity, we may normalize $\int f = 1$ without loss of
generality, so that $C_{1a} = \inf_{f} \|f*f\|_{L^\infty}$ over
nonnegative $f$ with $\supp(f) \subseteq [-\tfrac14, \tfrac14]$ and
$\int f = 1$.

\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Why this constant matters.}  $C_{1a}$ is connected to
the asymptotic size of Sidon sets in additive combinatorics.  A
\emph{Sidon set} is a set of integers where all pairwise sums are
distinct; equivalently, the ``representation function'' of the sumset
has values at most~2 everywhere except at doubled elements.  The
constant $C_{1a}$ governs how efficiently mass can be distributed to
minimize the peak of the autoconvolution --- the continuous analog of
controlling pairwise-sum collisions.

\subsection{Known Bounds}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcl}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Bound} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Reference} \\
  \midrule
  Upper bound & $\le 1.5029$ & Matolcsi \& Vinuesa (2010) \\
  Lower bound & $\ge 1.2802$ & Cloninger \& Steinerberger (2017) \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The \emph{upper bound} comes from exhibiting explicit functions whose
autoconvolution peak is small.  This is a constructive task: find a
good $f$ and compute its peak.

The \emph{lower bound} is fundamentally harder: one must show that
\emph{no} function can achieve a peak below the threshold.  This
requires exhaustive elimination of all possibilities, which is the
subject of this document.

\subsection{Proof Strategy at a High Level}

The proof proceeds in three conceptual steps:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Reduce} the infinite-dimensional optimization
    (over all nonnegative functions) to a finite-dimensional one
    (over vectors of bin averages), with a quantified approximation
    error.

  \item \textbf{Discretize} the finite-dimensional simplex to a
    lattice, with a quantified rounding error.

  \item \textbf{Enumerate} every lattice point and verify that each
    one has autoconvolution peak exceeding the target threshold.
    If all are eliminated, the bound is proven.
\end{enumerate}

Step~(iii) is made tractable by pruning strategies (asymmetry,
windowed bounds) and a multiscale cascade (coarse-to-fine refinement).

\section{From Functions to Bin Averages}\label{sec:bin}
% ======================================================================

Our first order of business is to replace the infinite-dimensional
optimization --- over all nonnegative functions --- with something
finite-dimensional and computable.

\subsection{The Partition}

Fix a positive integer $n$ (the ``half-bin count'').  Partition
$(-\tfrac14, \tfrac14)$ into $d = 2n$ equal bins:
\[
  I_j \;=\; \left(\frac{j}{4n},\; \frac{j+1}{4n}\right),
  \qquad j = -n, -n+1, \ldots, n-1.
\]
Each bin has width $\tfrac{1}{4n}$.

\begin{remark}
We use $d = 2n$ bins total, indexed from $-n$ to $n-1$.  In the
implementation, we re-index these as $0, 1, \ldots, d-1$ (i.e.,
$j \mapsto j + n$).  The mathematics is the same either way.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Bin Averages}

For any nonnegative $f$ with $\supp(f) \subseteq (-\tfrac14,
\tfrac14)$ and $\int f = 1$, define the \emph{bin averages}:
\[
  a_j \;=\; 4n \int_{I_j} f(x)\, dx \;\ge\; 0,
  \qquad j = 0, \ldots, d-1.
\]

The factor $4n$ normalizes so that each $a_j$ equals the average value
of $f$ on the bin $I_j$ (since $|I_j| = \tfrac{1}{4n}$).  The
constraint $\int f = 1$ becomes
\[
  \sum_{j=0}^{d-1} a_j \;=\; 4n.
\]

\begin{definition}
The \emph{continuous simplex} is
\[
  A_n \;=\; \Bigl\{
    a \in \R_{\ge 0}^d
    \;\Big|\;
    \sum_{j=0}^{d-1} a_j = 4n
  \Bigr\}.
\]
\end{definition}

Every nonnegative function $f$ with $\int f = 1$ maps to a point in $A_n$,
but not every point in $A_n$ arises from a function (the map loses
information about the shape of $f$ within each bin).

\subsection{The Lower Bound on $\|f*f\|_\infty$}

Remarkably, the entire reduction rests on two elementary observations
--- one geometric, one analytic.

\begin{lemma}[Reduction to Bin Averages --- Lemma~1 of CS17]
\label{lem:bin-reduction}
For any $f \in \mathcal{F}$ with $\int f = 1$ and corresponding bin
averages $a = (a_0, \ldots, a_{d-1})$:
\[
  \|f * f\|_{L^\infty}
  \;\ge\;
  \max_{\substack{2 \le \ell \le 2d \\[2pt] k}}
  \frac{1}{4n\ell}
  \sum_{\substack{0 \le i, j \le d-1 \\[1pt] k \le i+j \le k+\ell-2}}
  a_i\, a_j.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\noindent\textbf{Ingredient 1: Support of cross-convolutions.}
Let $f_i = f \cdot \mathbf{1}_{I_i}$ be the restriction of $f$ to bin
$I_i$.  Then $f = \sum_i f_i$ and
\[
  (f * f)(t) \;=\; \sum_{i,j} (f_i * f_j)(t).
\]
The convolution $f_i * f_j$ is supported on the Minkowski sum
$I_i + I_j$, which is an interval of width $\tfrac{1}{2n}$.
The key point: \emph{the index $i + j$ determines which part of the
convolution domain receives the contribution from $f_i * f_j$}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Ingredient 2: Pigeonhole.}
For any nonnegative function $h$ and interval $J$:
$\|h\|_{L^\infty} \ge \tfrac{1}{|J|} \int_J h$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Combining.}  Consider a ``window'' covering
sum-indices $s \in \{k, k+1, \ldots, k + \ell - 2\}$.  The Minkowski
sums for consecutive sum-indices overlap (their centers are spaced
$\tfrac{1}{4n}$ apart, each of width $\tfrac{1}{2n}$), so their
union forms an interval of length exactly $\tfrac{\ell}{4n}$ in the
convolution domain.  All cross-terms $f_i * f_j$ with
$k \le i + j \le k + \ell - 2$ have support within this interval,
contributing total $L^1$ mass
$\sum \tfrac{a_i}{4n} \cdot \tfrac{a_j}{4n}$.  Applying the
pigeonhole bound:
\[
  \|f * f\|_{L^\infty}
  \;\ge\;
  \frac{1}{\ell / (4n)}
  \sum_{\substack{k \le i+j \le k+\ell-2}}
  \frac{a_i\, a_j}{(4n)^2}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{4n\ell}
  \sum_{\substack{k \le i+j \le k+\ell-2}}
  a_i\, a_j.
\]
Taking the maximum over all windows gives the result.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Intuition]
Think of the bin averages $a_i$ as describing ``how much mass $f$ puts
in each bin.''  The products $a_i a_j$ measure how much the mass in
bins $i$ and $j$ contribute to the autoconvolution.  The window
argument says: if too much product-mass falls in any fixed interval of
the convolution domain, then $f * f$ must be large somewhere in that
interval.

The reader may wonder: why not just take the narrowest possible window
($\ell = 2$) everywhere?  Because narrow windows see fewer
cross-terms, so their sums can be small even when mass is spread
across many bins.  The interplay between window width and the number of
contributing pairs is what makes the optimization nontrivial.
\end{remark}


% ======================================================================
\section{Discretization of the Simplex}\label{sec:disc}
% ======================================================================

Having reduced the problem to minimizing over the continuous simplex
$A_n$, we face a new obstacle: $A_n$ is uncountable.  To bring the
problem within reach of a computer, we approximate $A_n$ by a finite
lattice, paying a small but quantifiable price in accuracy.

\subsection{The Lattice}

To discretize $A_n$, we need a grid fine enough to approximate any
point in the simplex, yet coarse enough to enumerate.  The natural
choice is a lattice of spacing $1/m$.

\begin{definition}
Fix a positive integer $m$ (the ``grid resolution'').  The
\emph{discrete lattice} is
\[
  B_{n,m} \;=\; \left\{
    b \in \left(\tfrac{1}{m}\N\right)^d
    \;\middle|\;
    \sum_{j=0}^{d-1} b_j = 4n
  \right\},
\]
where each $b_j$ is a nonnegative multiple of $\tfrac{1}{m}$.
\end{definition}

Equivalently, writing $c_j = m \cdot b_j$, the lattice consists of
nonnegative integer vectors $c = (c_0, \ldots, c_{d-1})$ with
$\sum c_j = S$, where $S = 4nm$.  These are the \emph{compositions}
of the integer $S$ into $d$ nonnegative parts.

\begin{remark}[Implementation convention]
The implementation uses a rescaled convention where integer coordinates
sum to $m$ rather than $4nm$, absorbing the factor $4n$ into the
scale when converting to bin averages: $a_j = c_j \cdot 4n / m$.  The
mathematics is equivalent; we use the $S = 4nm$ convention in this
proof for consistency with the bin-average normalization.
\end{remark}

The lattice is useful only if every continuous point has a nearby
lattice point --- otherwise gaps in the lattice could hide the
minimizer.

\begin{lemma}[Lattice as an Approximation --- Lemma~2 of CS17]
\label{lem:lattice-approx}
$B_{n,m}$ is a $\tfrac{1}{m}$-net of $A_n$ in $\ell^\infty$-norm.
That is, for every $a \in A_n$, there exists $b \in B_{n,m}$ with
$\|a - b\|_\infty \le \tfrac{1}{m}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Given $a \in A_n$, round each coordinate to the nearest multiple of
$\tfrac{1}{m}$, adjusting to maintain the sum constraint.
Specifically, use a greedy rounding: process coordinates left to
right, maintaining a running deficit $t_i = \sum_{j<i}(b_j - a_j)$.
Round down first, then alternate to keep $-\tfrac{1}{m} < t_{i+1} \le
0$.  The last coordinate is set to enforce the exact sum.  Each
coordinate satisfies $|a_j - b_j| \le \tfrac{1}{m}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Discretization Error Bound}

Since we approximate $a$ by $b$ with per-coordinate error at most
$\tfrac{1}{m}$, the windowed test value changes by a bounded amount.

\begin{lemma}[Discretization Error --- Lemma~3 of CS17]
\label{lem:disc-error}
For any admissible window $(\ell,k)$, any $a \in A_n$, and any lattice
approximation $b \in B_{n,m}$ with $\|a-b\|_\infty \le \tfrac{1}{m}$,
\[
  W_{\ell,k}(a)
  \;\ge\;
  W_{\ell,k}(b)
  \;-\;
  \frac{4n}{\ell}\left(\frac{2}{m} + \frac{1}{m^2}\right),
\]
where
\[
  W_{\ell,k}(x)
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{4n\ell}
  \sum_{\substack{0 \le i,j \le d-1 \\ k \le i+j \le k+\ell-2}}
  x_i x_j.
\]
Consequently,
\[
  \tv(a)
  \;\ge\;
  \tv(b)
  \;-\;
  2n\left(\frac{2}{m} + \frac{1}{m^2}\right).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
This is the corrected CS17 discretization bound.  For a fixed window,
the perturbation $a=b+\varepsilon$ contributes two error terms: a cross
term of size $\tfrac{4n}{\ell}\cdot \tfrac{2}{m}\,W$, where $W \le 1$
is the total contributing mass in that window, and a quadratic term of
size $\tfrac{4n}{\ell}\cdot \tfrac{1}{m^2}$.  Summing them yields the
per-window correction
\[
  \frac{4n}{\ell}\left(\frac{2}{m} + \frac{1}{m^2}\right).
\]
Since $\ell \ge 2$, we have $\tfrac{4n}{\ell} \le 2n$, which gives the
stated global bound for $\tv$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The correction term in practice]
The raw CS17 correction is $\tfrac{2}{m} + \tfrac{1}{m^2}$, but the
global level-$n$ correction is
$2n\left(\tfrac{2}{m} + \tfrac{1}{m^2}\right)$.  At the starting level
$n=2$ and $m=20$, this is $0.41$.  In the current codebase, this
global quantity is used only for coarse caps and reporting; the actual
pruning kernel uses the sharper window-dependent threshold described
next.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Refined (Dynamic) Correction}

The global correction bound from Lemma~\ref{lem:disc-error} is
pessimistic: it assumes the worst-case window length and replaces the
actual contributing mass by its trivial upper bound.  A tighter,
position-dependent bound exploits the fact that narrow or
edge-positioned windows involve fewer bins.

\begin{lemma}[Dynamic Correction --- Equation~(1) of CS17]
\label{lem:dynamic-correction}
For a specific window at position $k$ with length $\ell$, let
$W_{\text{int}} = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}(k,\ell)} c_i$ be the total
integer mass in contributing bins, where $\mathcal{B}(k,\ell) = \{i :
\exists\, j \text{ with } k \le i+j \le k+\ell-2\}$.  Then the
\emph{window-specific effective threshold} is:
\[
  T_{\ell,k} \;=\; c_{\mathrm{target}}
    + \frac{4n}{\ell}\cdot \frac{1 + 2\, W_{\mathrm{int}}}{m^2}
\]
rather than the coarse global threshold
$c_{\mathrm{target}} + 2n\left(\tfrac{2}{m} + \tfrac{1}{m^2}\right)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $\mathrm{ws}$ be the integer window sum of the autoconvolution of a
composition $c$ with $\sum_i c_i = m$.  Since the implementation uses
the rescaling $a_i = \tfrac{4n}{m}c_i$, the corresponding test value is
\[
  W_{\ell,k}(a) \;=\; \frac{4n}{m^2\ell}\,\mathrm{ws}.
\]
Therefore the corrected dynamic pruning condition is equivalent to
\[
  \text{int\_thresh}
  \;=\;
  \bigl\lfloor
    \Bigl(
      c_{\mathrm{target}} \cdot m^2 \cdot \frac{\ell}{4n}
      + 1 + 2\,W_{\text{int}} + 10^{-9}m^2
    \Bigr)
    (1 - 4\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})
  \bigr\rfloor,
\]
which is the corrected form now used in
\texttt{run\_cascade.py}.  The
additive $10^{-9}m^2$ term raises the threshold, while the factor
$(1 - 4\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$ lowers it slightly before flooring;
the additive margin dominates, so the net effect is conservative.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why dynamic is better]
The dynamic correction keeps track of the actual contributing mass
$W_{\text{int}}$ and retains the favorable factor $\tfrac{4n}{\ell}$.
Long windows and edge windows therefore carry smaller corrections than
the worst-case global bound, which is why the cascade can prune far
more aggressively than a uniform-threshold search.
\end{remark}

\section{The Windowed Test Value}\label{sec:testvalue}
% ======================================================================

With the discretization machinery in hand, we can describe precisely
what the algorithm computes at each lattice point.

\subsection{Definition}

\begin{definition}
For a mass vector $a = (a_0, \ldots, a_{d-1})$ with $a_i \ge 0$ and
$\sum a_i = 4n$, the \emph{autoconvolution} is the vector of length
$2d - 1$:
\[
  \conv[s] \;=\; \sum_{\substack{i + j = s \\[1pt] 0 \le i,j \le d-1}}
  a_i\, a_j,
  \qquad s = 0, 1, \ldots, 2(d-1).
\]
The \emph{test value} is
\[
  \tv(a) \;=\;
  \max_{\substack{2 \le \ell \le 2d \\[2pt] 0 \le k \le 2(d-1) - (\ell-2)}}
  \frac{1}{4n\ell}
  \sum_{s=k}^{k+\ell-2} \conv[s].
\]
\end{definition}

In words: compute the autoconvolution, slide a window of each possible
width $\ell$ across it, sum the $\ell - 1$ consecutive entries in each
window position, normalize by $4n\ell$, and take the maximum.

\begin{remark}[Integer implementation coordinates]
The code stores compositions
$c=(c_0,\ldots,c_{d-1}) \in \N^d$ with $\sum_i c_i = m$ and recovers
the bin-average vector by
\[
  a_i \;=\; \frac{4n}{m}\,c_i.
\]
All pruning formulas below are stated in these implementation
coordinates.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Efficient Computation via Prefix Sums}

The test value can be computed efficiently using prefix sums.  Let
$P[s] = \sum_{k=0}^{s} \conv[k]$ be the prefix sum of the
autoconvolution.  Then the windowed sum from $s_{\text{lo}}$ to
$s_{\text{hi}}$ is:
\[
  \sum_{s=s_\text{lo}}^{s_\text{hi}} \conv[s]
  \;=\;
  P[s_\text{hi}] - P[s_\text{lo} - 1]
  \qquad (\text{with } P[-1] = 0).
\]

\begin{remark}[Complexity]
Computing the autoconvolution takes $O(d^2)$ time.  An FFT-based method
would give $O(d \log d)$, but for the small $d$ values used here the
direct method is faster in practice.  The prefix sum takes $O(d)$, and
the complete window scan takes another $O(d^2)$.  Thus the overall
cost per lattice point is $O(d^2)$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Reversal Symmetry}\label{subsec:reversal}

A key structural observation cuts the search space in half.  If we
reverse the order of the bins, the autoconvolution peak does not change
--- intuitively, because convolution does not care which direction we
label the support.

\begin{theorem}[Autoconvolution Reversal Symmetry]
\label{thm:reversal}
For any mass vector $a$, $\tv(a) = \tv(\rev(a))$, where
$\rev(a) = (a_{d-1}, \ldots, a_0)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The argument has three moving parts.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 1: Autoconvolution reversal.}
Let $a' = \rev(a)$, so $a'_i = a_{d-1-i}$.  Then:
\[
  \conv'[s]
  \;=\; \sum_{i+j=s} a'_i a'_j
  \;=\; \sum_{i+j=s} a_{d-1-i}\, a_{d-1-j}.
\]
Substituting $i' = d-1-i$, $j' = d-1-j$ (so $i'+j' = 2(d-1) - s$):
\[
  \conv'[s] \;=\; \conv[2(d-1) - s].
\]
That is, the autoconvolution of the reversal is the reversal of the
autoconvolution.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 2: Window-sum bijection.}
A window sum of width $w = \ell - 1$ starting at $s_0$ transforms
under reversal to a window sum of the same width starting at
$2(d-1) - s_0 - w + 1$:
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{s=s_0}^{s_0+w-1} \conv'[s]
  &= \sum_{s=s_0}^{s_0+w-1} \conv[2(d-1)-s] \\
  &= \sum_{s'=2(d-1)-s_0-w+1}^{2(d-1)-s_0} \conv[s'].
\end{align*}
This is a window of the same width $w$, so the multiset of all window
sums at width $w$ is identical for $\conv$ and $\conv'$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 3: Normalization preserves the maximum.}
The normalization $\tfrac{1}{4n\ell}$ depends only on $\ell$, not the
window position.  Since the multiset of window sums is identical at
each width, $\tv(a) = \tv(a')$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Canonical Symmetry]
It suffices to check only \emph{canonical} compositions $b$ satisfying
$b \le \rev(b)$ lexicographically.  This halves the search space (up
to palindromes).
\end{corollary}

Even after exploiting reversal symmetry, the number of lattice points
to check --- roughly $\binom{S+d-1}{d-1}/2$ --- grows rapidly with $d$
and $m$.  To keep the computation tractable, we need ways to rule out
large swathes of the simplex without computing the full test value.


% ======================================================================
\section{Pruning Strategies}\label{sec:pruning}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Asymmetry Pruning}

At first glance, the following bound might seem too crude to be useful:
it ignores everything about $f$ except how much mass sits on each half
of the support.  Yet in practice, this single test eliminates the vast
majority of configurations at every cascade level.

\begin{theorem}[Asymmetry Bound]
\label{thm:asymmetry}
Let $f \ge 0$ with $\supp(f) \subseteq (-\tfrac14, \tfrac14)$ and
$\int f = 1$.  Let $L = \int_{-1/4}^{0} f$ be the left-half mass.
Then:
\[
  \|f * f\|_{L^\infty} \;\ge\; 2L^2.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Consider the left-left convolution:
\[
  (f * f)(t) \;=\; \int f(x)\, f(t-x)\, dx
  \;\ge\; \int_{-1/4}^{0} f(x)\, f(t-x)\, \mathbf{1}_{[-1/4,0]}(t-x)\, dx
\]
for $t \in (-\tfrac12, 0)$.  By Fubini's theorem, the total mass of
$f * f$ restricted to $(-\tfrac12, 0)$ is at least $L^2$ (the
left-left contribution).  Since $|(-\tfrac12, 0)| = \tfrac12$:
\[
  \|f * f\|_{L^\infty} \;\ge\; \frac{L^2}{1/2} \;=\; 2L^2.
\]
By symmetry, the same holds for the right-half mass $R = 1 - L$:
$\|f*f\|_\infty \ge 2R^2$.  Therefore
$\|f*f\|_\infty \ge 2 \max(L^2, R^2)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Asymmetry Threshold]
\label{cor:asym-threshold}
If $2L^2 \ge c_{\mathrm{target}}$, i.e.,
$L \ge \sqrt{c_{\mathrm{target}}/2}$, then the configuration is
ruled out.  The threshold left-mass fraction is:
\[
  p^* \;=\; \sqrt{c_{\mathrm{target}} / 2}.
\]
For $c_{\mathrm{target}} = 1.4$: $p^* \approx 0.8367$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[No discrete margin needed]
One might expect a safety margin to account for the discrepancy between
discrete and continuous left-mass fractions.  However, our discrete
compositions represent piecewise-constant step functions, for which the
left-half mass $L = \sum_{i < n} c_i / m$ is \emph{exact} --- no
approximation is involved.  Moreover, the midpoint $x = 0$ always
falls on a bin boundary, and the left-mass fraction is preserved
exactly under dyadic refinement (child bins for the left half sum to
the parent's left bins).  Therefore no margin is necessary: we prune
at the exact threshold $p^*$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Important subtlety]
The asymmetry bound is a \emph{direct} $L^\infty$ bound on $f * f$.
It does \emph{not} go through the discretization framework of
Lemma~\ref{lem:disc-error}, so within the discrete cascade we compare
it against $c_{\mathrm{target}}$ itself, not against a
correction-shifted test-value threshold.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[What the current implementation actually prunes by]
Earlier drafts experimented with additional block-sum and two-peak
heuristics.  The current CPU cascade does not rely on them.  The
proof-critical pruning rules in the present code are asymmetry pruning,
the corrected window-dependent dynamic threshold, reversal
canonicalization, and the single-bin cap used during child generation.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Canonical Symmetry Exploitation}

By Theorem~\ref{thm:reversal}, $\tv(b) = \tv(\rev(b))$ for every
lattice point $b$.  This motivates the following notion of canonicality.

\begin{definition}
A composition $b$ is \emph{canonical} if $b \le \rev(b)$
lexicographically.  That is, comparing $b_0$ with $b_{d-1}$, then
$b_1$ with $b_{d-2}$, etc., $b$ comes first (or they are equal, i.e.,
$b$ is a palindrome).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Canonical Count]
\label{prop:canon-count}
The number of canonical compositions is $\tfrac{N + P}{2}$, where
$N = \binom{S+d-1}{d-1}$ is the total count and $P$ is the number of
palindromes.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Every composition falls into exactly one of three categories:
palindromes (those equal to their own reversal), strictly canonical
non-palindromes ($b < \rev(b)$), and their reversals.  Since reversal
is an involution with no fixed points outside the palindromes, the
second and third categories have equal size $C$.  Counting gives
$N = P + 2C$, so the canonical count is $P + C = (N+P)/2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Loop-bound tightening for $d = 4$]
For $b = (c_0, c_1, c_2, c_3)$ with $c_3 = S - c_0 - c_1 - c_2$,
canonicality ($b \le \rev(b)$) requires $c_0 \le c_3$, giving three
loop bounds:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $c_0 \le \lfloor S/2 \rfloor$ \quad (if $c_0 > S/2$ then
    $c_3 < c_0$).
  \item $c_1 \le S - 2c_0$ \quad (need $c_2 \ge 0$ with
    $c_1 + c_2 \le S - 2c_0$).
  \item $c_2 \le S - 2c_0 - c_1$ \quad (ensures $c_3 \ge c_0$).
\end{itemize}
Plus a palindrome filter: when $c_0 = c_3$, require $c_1 \le c_2$.

For $d = 6$, analogous bounds apply: $c_0 \le \lfloor S/2 \rfloor$,
$c_4 \le S - c_0 - c_1 - c_2 - c_3 - c_0$, with equality checks for
the inner pairs when outer pairs tie.
\end{remark}

\section{The Multiscale Cascade}\label{sec:cascade}
% ======================================================================

Brute-force enumeration at fine resolution is hopelessly infeasible ---
even modest parameters yield billions of lattice points.  The key idea,
borrowed from multigrid methods in numerical analysis, is to start
coarse: most configurations can be ruled out cheaply at low resolution,
and only the stubborn survivors need to be examined more closely.

\subsection{Overview}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Step \arabic*:}]
  \item \textbf{Start coarse.}  At level $L_0$, enumerate all
    canonical compositions in $B_{n_0, m}$ with $d_0 = 2n_0$ bins
    (e.g., $n_0 = 2$, $d_0 = 4$).

  \item \textbf{Prune.}  For each composition, apply asymmetry
    pruning and then scan windows using the corrected dynamic
    threshold.  If some window certifies the target bound, the
    composition is eliminated.

  \item \textbf{Refine survivors.}  Each surviving composition at
    resolution $d$ is refined to resolution $2d$ by \emph{dyadic
    splitting}: each bin is split into two sub-bins.

  \item \textbf{Repeat.}  Prune the refined compositions.  Continue
    until either all are eliminated (proof complete) or a
    computational budget is reached.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Dyadic Splitting (Refinement)}

Given a parent composition $b = (b_0, \ldots, b_{d-1})$ at resolution
$d$, the refinement produces children $c = (c_0, c_1, \ldots,
c_{2d-1})$ at resolution $2d$, where:
\[
  c_{2i} + c_{2i+1} \;=\; b_i \qquad \text{for each } i = 0, \ldots, d-1,
\]
with $c_{2i}, c_{2i+1} \ge 0$ and both being nonnegative integers.

\subsection{Energy Cap}\label{subsec:energy-cap}

One might worry that generating all possible children is wasteful.
Indeed, many children are immediately ruled out by the single-bin
diagonal window.  The energy cap avoids generating them in the first
place.

Recall that $a_j = \tfrac{4n_{\text{child}}}{m}c_j$ and the $\ell = 2$ diagonal window at
position $2j$ gives
\[
  \tv(a) \;\ge\; d_{\text{child}}\,\frac{c_j^2}{m^2},
  \qquad d_{\text{child}} = 2n_{\text{child}}.
\]
This yields the test-value cap
\[
  x_{\text{cap}}^{\mathrm{TV}}
  \;=\;
  \left\lfloor
    m \sqrt{\frac{c_{\mathrm{target}} +
      2n_{\text{child}}\left(\tfrac{2}{m} + \tfrac{1}{m^2}\right)
      + 10^{-9}}
    {d_{\text{child}}}}
  \right\rfloor.
\]
The implementation also applies the sharper direct cap
\[
  x_{\text{cap}}^{\mathrm{CS}}
  \;=\;
  \left\lfloor
    m \sqrt{\frac{c_{\mathrm{target}}}{d_{\text{child}}}}
  \right\rfloor,
\]
and uses
$x_{\text{cap}} = \min\!\left(x_{\text{cap}}^{\mathrm{TV}},
x_{\text{cap}}^{\mathrm{CS}}\right)$.

\begin{proposition}[Energy Cap Soundness]
If a child has some bin $c_j > x_{\mathrm{cap}}^{\mathrm{CS}}$, then
any nonnegative function with those child-bin masses already satisfies
$\|f*f\|_{L^\infty} > c_{\mathrm{target}}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $M_j = c_j/m$ be the mass in the $j$th child bin.  Restricting $f$
to that single bin produces a nonnegative function $f_j$ supported on
an interval of length $1/d_{\text{child}}$ and with total mass $M_j$.
By the same averaging argument used for asymmetry pruning,
\[
  \|f*f\|_{L^\infty}
  \;\ge\;
  \|f_j*f_j\|_{L^\infty}
  \;\ge\;
  \frac{M_j^2}{1/d_{\text{child}}}
  \;=\;
  d_{\text{child}}\,\frac{c_j^2}{m^2}.
\]
If
$c_j > m \sqrt{c_{\mathrm{target}}/d_{\text{child}}}$,
the right-hand side exceeds $c_{\mathrm{target}}$, which proves the
claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Tighter Cauchy--Schwarz cap]
The looser cap $x_{\text{cap}}^{\mathrm{TV}}$ mirrors the older global
correction narrative, while the direct cap
$x_{\text{cap}}^{\mathrm{CS}}$ is the effective one in the current
implementation.  It is tighter and does not need any discretization
correction term.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Cascade Soundness}

\begin{theorem}[Cascade Soundness]
\label{thm:cascade-sound}
If the cascade terminates with zero survivors at some level $L_k$
(with resolution $d_k = d_0 \cdot 2^k$), then
$C_{1a} \ge c_{\mathrm{target}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof outline]
The content of the cascade is not merely that ``no survivor remains'';
it is that every terminal discrete configuration has acquired a
certificate.  Concretely, each configuration is eliminated either by a
direct asymmetry certificate or by a specific window certificate
$(\ell,s_{\mathrm{lo}})$ satisfying the corrected dynamic threshold of
Lemma~\ref{lem:dynamic-correction}.  Completeness of refinement and
canonical handling guarantees that every composition at the terminal
resolution is represented by one of these certificates.  The asymmetry
certificates are direct $L^\infty$ lower bounds, while the window
certificates combine with Lemma~\ref{lem:disc-error} to transfer the
bound back to the continuous problem.  Hence, once the terminal
survivor set is empty, every admissible profile has been ruled out
below $c_{\mathrm{target}}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Canonical Handling Across Levels}

At level $L_0$, only canonical compositions are generated.  At levels
$L_1$ and beyond, the situation is more subtle:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Children of a canonical parent $P$ are \emph{not} necessarily
    canonical.
  \item The reversal $\rev(P)$ is \emph{not} in the parent list
    (unless $P$ is a palindrome), so its children are not generated
    directly.
  \item However, if $C$ is a child of $P$, then $\rev(C)$ is a child
    of $\rev(P)$.  Since $\tv(C) = \tv(\rev(C))$
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:reversal}), testing $C$ suffices for both.
\end{itemize}

Therefore at levels $\ge 1$:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Generate \emph{all} children of each parent (no canonical
    filter during generation).
  \item Test each child against the dynamic threshold.
  \item \emph{Canonicalize} survivors: replace each with
    $\min(C, \rev(C))$ lexicographically.
  \item \emph{Deduplicate}: remove duplicate canonical forms (children
    from different parents, or a palindrome parent producing both $C$
    and $\rev(C)$).
\end{enumerate}

\begin{proposition}[Completeness of Canonical Handling]
This procedure tests every composition at the child resolution:
if $C'$ is any child-level composition and $P' = \text{parent}(C')$,
then either $P'$ or $\rev(P')$ is in the parent list, and $C'$ or
$\rev(C')$ is among the tested children.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Fused Generate-and-Prune Kernel}

At deep cascade levels, each parent may have millions of children.
The fused kernel generates and tests each child inline via a
Cartesian-product odometer, never materializing the full children
array:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Incremental autoconvolution:} when only the last
    cursor position changes, the raw
    autoconvolution is updated in $O(d)$ instead of $O(d^2)$.
  \item \textbf{Quick-check:} re-tries the previous killing window
    before a full scan.
  \item \textbf{Subtree pruning:} on deep carries, checks the
    partial autoconvolution of fixed bins against worst-case unfixed
    mass; if it exceeds the threshold, the entire subtree is skipped.
\end{itemize}

\section{The Complete Proof}\label{sec:algorithm}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Prove $C_{1a} \ge c_{\text{target}}$}
\label{alg:cascade}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $c_{\text{target}}$, grid resolution $m$, starting half-bins
  $n_0$, max levels $L_{\max}$
\State $d_0 \gets 2 n_0$
\State Generate all canonical compositions of $m$ into $d_0$ parts
  \Comment{implementation coordinates}
\State Apply asymmetry pruning (threshold
  $\sqrt{c_{\text{target}}/2}$)
\State For every remaining composition, scan all windows and prune if
  some window exceeds the dynamic threshold from
  Lemma~\ref{lem:dynamic-correction}
\State $\text{survivors}_0 \gets$ unpruned compositions
\For{$k = 1, 2, \ldots, L_{\max}$}
  \State $d_k \gets 2 d_{k-1}$; \quad
    $n_k \gets 2 n_{k-1}$
  \State Compute the single-bin child cap
    $x_{\text{cap}} = \min(x_{\text{cap}}^{\mathrm{TV}},
    x_{\text{cap}}^{\mathrm{CS}})$
  \ForAll{parent $P \in \text{survivors}_{k-1}$}
    \ForAll{children $C$ of $P$ (dyadic split with energy cap)}
      \State Apply asymmetry pruning to $C$
      \If{not asymmetry-pruned}
        \State Scan all windows of $C$ via prefix-sum autoconvolution
        \If{some window exceeds the dynamic threshold}
          \State Prune $C$
        \Else
          \State $C$ survives; canonicalize and store
        \EndIf
      \EndIf
    \EndFor
  \EndFor
  \State Deduplicate survivors (canonicalize $\to$ sort $\to$ scan)
  \State $\text{survivors}_k \gets$ deduplicated survivors
  \If{$|\text{survivors}_k| = 0$}
    \State \Return \textsc{Proven}: $C_{1a} \ge c_{\text{target}}$
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return \textsc{Inconclusive}:
  $|\text{survivors}_{L_{\max}}|$ compositions remain
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Main Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Main Result]
\label{thm:main}
If Algorithm~\ref{alg:cascade} terminates with the status
\textnormal{\textsc{Proven}}, then
$C_{1a} \ge c_{\mathrm{target}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Combine three ingredients:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item the reduction from continuous functions to windowed test values
    (Lemma~\ref{lem:bin-reduction});
  \item the corrected discretization and dynamic-threshold bounds
    (Lemmas~\ref{lem:disc-error} and
    \ref{lem:dynamic-correction}); and
  \item the cascade completeness statement summarized in
    Theorem~\ref{thm:cascade-sound}.
\end{enumerate}
If Algorithm~\ref{alg:cascade} returns \textsc{Proven}, then some level
has zero survivors, so every configuration at that terminal resolution
has been eliminated by a valid asymmetry or window certificate.  Those
certificates are exactly the hypotheses needed to conclude that no
admissible $f$ can satisfy $\|f*f\|_\infty < c_{\mathrm{target}}$.
Therefore $C_{1a} \ge c_{\mathrm{target}}$.
\end{proof}

\section{Correctness Invariants}\label{sec:correctness}
% ======================================================================

Stating the algorithm is only half the battle.  For the proof to carry
mathematical weight, we must verify that the implementation faithfully
realizes the specification.  Four invariants pin down exactly what
``faithful'' means.

\subsection{Invariant 1: Complete Enumeration}

Every composition $b \in B_{n,m}$ must be either directly checked or
shown to be covered by a checked ancestor and all its refinements.

\begin{itemize}
  \item At $L_0$: all $\binom{m + d_0 - 1}{d_0 - 1}$ compositions
    are enumerated (in canonical form, with reversal symmetry).
    Verified by comparing the generator output count against the
    stars-and-bars formula, and by checking completeness, uniqueness,
    non-negativity, and sum constraints.
  \item At $L_k$ ($k \ge 1$): all children of all surviving parents
    are generated (no sampling or probabilistic skipping).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Invariant 2: All Windows Checked}

For each composition that is not eliminated by asymmetry, every valid
window $(\ell, k)$ with $2 \le \ell \le 2d$ and all valid starting
positions $k$ must be tested before the composition is declared a
survivor.  Early exit occurs only \emph{after} a pruning window is
found --- never for survivors.

\subsection{Invariant 3: Conservative Arithmetic}

Floating-point arithmetic is the Achilles' heel of computer-assisted
proofs: a single rounding error in the wrong direction could cause a
valid configuration to be falsely pruned, invalidating the entire
argument.  We guard against this as follows:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Integer coordinates: the autoconvolution is computed in
    integer arithmetic ($c_i c_j$ are exact integers), avoiding
    round-off entirely.
  \item Conservative threshold: the code evaluates the corrected
    integer threshold
    \[
      c_{\text{target}}m^2\frac{\ell}{4n} + 1 + 2W_{\text{int}},
    \]
    together with an added $10^{-9}m^2$ margin and a final
    $(1 - 4\epsilon_{\text{mach}})$ factor before flooring.
  \item The multiplicative factor lowers the threshold slightly, but
    the additive $10^{-9}m^2$ margin is overwhelmingly larger, so the
    net effect remains conservative: the code may miss a borderline
    prune, but it does not falsely prune.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Invariant 4: Refinement Completeness}

Every refinement of a surviving parent must be checked at the finer
level.  The dyadic splitting with energy cap generates all valid
children (Section~\ref{subsec:energy-cap}), and children exceeding the
energy cap are provably pruned.

\section{Computational Verification}\label{sec:verification}
% ======================================================================

The correctness of the implementation has been verified through
multiple independent checks.

\subsection{Composition Generators}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Completeness}: For 7 parameter combinations $(d, S)$,
    the generator output count matches $\binom{S+d-1}{d-1}$ exactly.
    All outputs sum to $S$, are non-negative, and are unique.
  \item \textbf{Specialized $\equiv$ generic}: The $d=4$ and $d=6$
    specialized generators produce identical sets to the generic
    stack-based generator.
  \item \textbf{Canonical correctness}: For 8 parameter combinations,
    canonical generators produce exactly $\tfrac{N+P}{2}$ outputs, all
    satisfying $b \le \rev(b)$, with every non-canonical $b$ having
    $\rev(b)$ in the output.
  \item \textbf{Batch boundaries}: Across 9 configurations and 8
    batch sizes, output is identical regardless of where batch
    boundaries fall (160 checks, all pass).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Test Value Computation}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Reversal symmetry ($\tv(b) = \tv(\rev(b))$) verified
    exhaustively for $d=4, S=8$ (165 compositions) and $d=6, S=6$
    (462 compositions), plus 1,500 random tests.  Zero mismatches.
  \item Pure-Python reference implementation agrees with Numba JIT
    kernel on all 891 canonical compositions at $d=4, m=20$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Pruning Correctness}

\begin{itemize}
  \item No over-pruning: zero compositions pruned whose true test
    value is below the dynamic threshold.
  \item No missed prunes: zero compositions not pruned whose true
    test value exceeds the dynamic threshold.
  \item Asymmetry filter verified to be exactly symmetric under
    reversal.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{MATLAB Baseline Comparison}

The cascade prover's dynamic threshold formula has been verified
algebraically equivalent to the original MATLAB code of
Cloninger--Steinerberger, with the Python implementation being
strictly more conservative (harder to prune) due to the explicit
$10^{-9}m^2$ safety margin.

\subsection{Lean Formalization Status}

This repository also contains a consolidated Lean 4 development in
\path{lean/complete_proof.lean}.  It formalizes a substantial portion
of the discrete infrastructure used by the cascade: foundational
discretization lemmas, reversal symmetry, composition counting,
refinement mass preservation, asymmetry lemmas, and several kernel
invariants related to $W_{\mathrm{int}}$, pruning monotonicity, and
integer arithmetic.

At the same time, the Lean file is not yet a completely end-to-end
formalization of the final theorem.  In particular, the corrected
per-window discretization bound enters there as an assumed lemma, and
the final cascade-to-continuous reduction remains only partially
formalized.  The TeX proof and the Lean development should therefore be
viewed as complementary rather than identical artifacts.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Relationship to the Original CS17 Proof}

Our framework is a direct extension of Cloninger \& Steinerberger
(2017).  The key differences:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Dynamic correction}: We use a sharper per-window
    correction rather than only a coarse global bound.  This improves
    pruning while preserving rigor.

  \item \textbf{Multiscale cascade}: CS17 used $m = 50$ and refined
    from $n = 3$ through $n = 24$.  We use $m = 20$ with dyadic
    refinement from $d = 4$ through $d = 64$, exploiting the fused
    kernel's efficiency.

  \item \textbf{Fused generation}: Instead of materializing all
    children and then testing, we generate and test each child inline,
    reducing memory from $O(N_{\text{children}} \cdot d)$ to $O(d)$
    per parent.

  \item \textbf{Integer arithmetic}: Autoconvolution and threshold
    comparisons use exact integer operations, with floating-point
    involved only in the conservative threshold computation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item The proof is \emph{computer-assisted}: the mathematical
    argument reduces correctness to a finite computation, but that
    computation must be performed by a machine.  The invariants in
    Section~\ref{sec:correctness} ensure that the computation is
    trustworthy.

  \item The approach is limited by the exponential growth of the
    composition count with $d$ and $m$.  The multiscale cascade
    mitigates this, but very aggressive targets
    ($c_{\mathrm{target}}$ close to $C_{1a}$) will require more
    cascade levels and higher computational cost.
\end{itemize}
