\title{%
  A Rigorous Proof Framework for Lower Bounds on the\\
  Autoconvolution Constant $C_{1a}$\\[6pt]
  \large Via the Cloninger--Steinerberger Branch-and-Prune Algorithm
}
\author{Jai Bajaj}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We present a self-contained, rigorous proof that the autoconvolution
constant $C_{1a}$ --- defined as the infimum of
$\|f * f\|_{L^\infty} / \bigl(\int f\bigr)^2$ over all nonnegative
functions supported on $[-\tfrac14, \tfrac14]$ --- satisfies a lower
bound that can be certified by exhaustive computation.  The proof
follows the framework of Cloninger and Steinerberger (2017), extended
with a multiscale cascade and tighter pruning.  We develop the
mathematics from first principles: the reduction from continuous
functions to finite-dimensional bin averages (Section~\ref{sec:bin}),
the discretization of the simplex and its error bound
(Section~\ref{sec:disc}), the windowed test-value criterion
(Section~\ref{sec:testvalue}), the pruning strategies that make
exhaustive search tractable (Section~\ref{sec:pruning}), the
multiscale cascade that exploits coarse-to-fine refinement
(Section~\ref{sec:cascade}), and the correctness invariants that
guarantee mathematical validity (Section~\ref{sec:correctness}).
Throughout, we emphasize intuition alongside formal statements.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{The Constant $C_{1a}$}

Consider the class of nonnegative, integrable functions supported on
the interval $[-\tfrac14, \tfrac14]$:
\[
  \mathcal{F} \;=\; \Bigl\{
    f : \R \to \R_{\ge 0}
    \;\Big|\;
    \supp(f) \subseteq \bigl[-\tfrac14, \tfrac14\bigr],\;
    \int f > 0
  \Bigr\}.
\]

The \emph{autoconvolution} of $f$ is
\[
  (f * f)(t) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\, f(t - x)\, dx.
\]
Since $\supp(f) \subseteq [-\tfrac14, \tfrac14]$, the
autoconvolution is supported on $[-\tfrac12, \tfrac12]$ and is itself
nonnegative.

\begin{definition}
The \emph{autoconvolution constant} is
\[
  C_{1a} \;=\; \inf_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \;
  \frac{\displaystyle\max_{|t| \le 1/2}\, (f*f)(t)}%
       {\displaystyle\left(\int f\right)^{\!2}}.
\]
\end{definition}

By homogeneity, we may normalize $\int f = 1$ without loss of
generality, so that $C_{1a} = \inf_{f} \|f*f\|_{L^\infty}$ over
nonnegative $f$ with $\supp(f) \subseteq [-\tfrac14, \tfrac14]$ and
$\int f = 1$.

\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Why this constant matters.}  $C_{1a}$ is connected to
the asymptotic size of Sidon sets in additive combinatorics.  A
\emph{Sidon set} is a set of integers where all pairwise sums are
distinct; equivalently, the ``representation function'' of the sumset
has values at most~2 everywhere except at doubled elements.  The
constant $C_{1a}$ governs how efficiently mass can be distributed to
minimize the peak of the autoconvolution --- the continuous analog of
controlling pairwise-sum collisions.

\subsection{Known Bounds}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcl}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Bound} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Reference} \\
  \midrule
  Upper bound & $\le 1.5029$ & Matolcsi \& Vinuesa (2010) \\
  Lower bound & $\ge 1.2802$ & Cloninger \& Steinerberger (2017) \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The \emph{upper bound} comes from exhibiting explicit functions whose
autoconvolution peak is small.  This is a constructive task: find a
good $f$ and compute its peak.

The \emph{lower bound} is fundamentally harder: one must show that
\emph{no} function can achieve a peak below the threshold.  This
requires exhaustive elimination of all possibilities, which is the
subject of this document.

\subsection{Proof Strategy at a High Level}

The proof proceeds in three conceptual steps:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Reduce} the infinite-dimensional optimization
    (over all nonneg functions) to a finite-dimensional one
    (over vectors of bin averages), with a quantified approximation
    error.

  \item \textbf{Discretize} the finite-dimensional simplex to a
    lattice, with a quantified rounding error.

  \item \textbf{Enumerate} every lattice point and verify that each
    one has autoconvolution peak exceeding the target threshold.
    If all are eliminated, the bound is proven.
\end{enumerate}

Step~(iii) is made tractable by pruning strategies (asymmetry,
windowed bounds) and a multiscale cascade (coarse-to-fine refinement).


% ======================================================================
\section{From Functions to Bin Averages}\label{sec:bin}
% ======================================================================

The first step reduces the problem from an optimization over functions
to an optimization over finite-dimensional vectors.

\subsection{The Partition}

Fix a positive integer $n$ (the ``half-bin count'').  Partition
$(-\tfrac14, \tfrac14)$ into $d = 2n$ equal bins:
\[
  I_j \;=\; \left(\frac{j}{4n},\; \frac{j+1}{4n}\right),
  \qquad j = -n, -n+1, \ldots, n-1.
\]
Each bin has width $\tfrac{1}{4n}$.

\begin{remark}
We use $d = 2n$ bins total, indexed from $-n$ to $n-1$.  In the
implementation, we re-index these as $0, 1, \ldots, d-1$ (i.e.,
$j \mapsto j + n$).  The mathematics is the same either way.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Bin Averages}

For any nonnegative $f$ with $\supp(f) \subseteq (-\tfrac14,
\tfrac14)$ and $\int f = 1$, define the \emph{bin averages}:
\[
  a_j \;=\; 4n \int_{I_j} f(x)\, dx \;\ge\; 0,
  \qquad j = 0, \ldots, d-1.
\]

The factor $4n$ normalizes so that each $a_j$ equals the average value
of $f$ on the bin $I_j$ (since $|I_j| = \tfrac{1}{4n}$).  The
constraint $\int f = 1$ becomes
\[
  \sum_{j=0}^{d-1} a_j \;=\; 4n.
\]

\begin{definition}
The \emph{continuous simplex} is
\[
  A_n \;=\; \Bigl\{
    a \in \R_{\ge 0}^d
    \;\Big|\;
    \sum_{j=0}^{d-1} a_j = 4n
  \Bigr\}.
\]
\end{definition}

Every nonneg function $f$ with $\int f = 1$ maps to a point in $A_n$,
but not every point in $A_n$ arises from a function (the map loses
information about the shape of $f$ within each bin).

\subsection{The Lower Bound on $\|f*f\|_\infty$}

The key result is that the autoconvolution peak can be lower-bounded
using only the bin averages.

\begin{lemma}[Reduction to Bin Averages --- Lemma~1 of CS17]
\label{lem:bin-reduction}
For any $f \in \mathcal{F}$ with $\int f = 1$ and corresponding bin
averages $a = (a_0, \ldots, a_{d-1})$:
\[
  \|f * f\|_{L^\infty}
  \;\ge\;
  \max_{\substack{2 \le \ell \le 2n \\[2pt] k}}
  \frac{1}{4n\ell}
  \sum_{\substack{0 \le i, j \le d-1 \\[1pt] k \le i+j \le k+\ell-2}}
  a_i\, a_j.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The proof uses two ingredients:

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Ingredient 1: Support of cross-convolutions.}
Let $f_i = f \cdot \mathbf{1}_{I_i}$ be the restriction of $f$ to bin
$I_i$.  Then $f = \sum_i f_i$ and
\[
  (f * f)(t) \;=\; \sum_{i,j} (f_i * f_j)(t).
\]
The convolution $f_i * f_j$ is supported on the Minkowski sum
$I_i + I_j$, which is an interval of width $\tfrac{2}{4n} =
\tfrac{1}{2n}$ centered at $\tfrac{i+j+1}{4n}$.  The key point:
\emph{the index $i + j$ determines which part of the convolution
domain receives the contribution from the cross-term $f_i * f_j$}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Ingredient 2: Pigeonhole / $L^\infty$--$L^1$ bound.}
For any nonneg function $h$ and interval $J$:
\[
  \|h\|_{L^\infty} \;\ge\; \frac{1}{|J|} \int_J h.
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Combining.}  Consider a ``window'' of $\ell$
consecutive bins in the convolution domain, starting at position $k$.
The convolution-domain bin for sum-index $s = i + j$ has width
$\tfrac{1}{2n}$.  A window covering $\ell - 1$ consecutive
sum-indices $s \in \{k, k+1, \ldots, k + \ell - 2\}$ spans a
convolution-domain interval of length $\tfrac{\ell}{2n}$.

All cross-terms $f_i * f_j$ with $k \le i + j \le k + \ell - 2$ have
their support within this window.  Each cross-term contributes
$\int f_i * f_j = (\int f_i)(\int f_j) = \tfrac{a_i}{4n} \cdot
\tfrac{a_j}{4n}$ total mass.  Since $f * f$ is nonneg, the total
$L^1$ mass of $f * f$ on this window is at least the sum of these
cross-term contributions.  Applying the pigeonhole bound:
\[
  \|f * f\|_{L^\infty}
  \;\ge\;
  \frac{1}{\ell / (2n)}
  \sum_{\substack{k \le i+j \le k+\ell-2}}
  \frac{a_i\, a_j}{(4n)^2}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{4n\ell}
  \sum_{\substack{k \le i+j \le k+\ell-2}}
  a_i\, a_j.
\]
Taking the maximum over all windows gives the result.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Intuition]
Think of the bin averages $a_i$ as describing ``how much mass $f$ puts
in each bin.''  The products $a_i a_j$ measure how much the mass in
bins $i$ and $j$ contribute to the autoconvolution.  The window
argument says: if too much product-mass falls in any fixed interval of
the convolution domain, then $f * f$ must be large somewhere in that
interval.  The smaller the window $\ell$, the stronger the conclusion
(mass is concentrated in a smaller region), but the fewer terms
contribute to the sum.
\end{remark}


% ======================================================================
\section{Discretization of the Simplex}\label{sec:disc}
% ======================================================================

Lemma~\ref{lem:bin-reduction} reduced the problem to minimizing over
the continuous simplex $A_n$.  But $A_n$ is an uncountable set.  We
now discretize it to a finite lattice, at the cost of a small
correction term.

\subsection{The Lattice}

Fix a positive integer $m$ (the ``grid resolution'').

\begin{definition}
The \emph{discrete lattice} is
\[
  B_{n,m} \;=\; \left\{
    b \in \left(\tfrac{1}{m}\N\right)^d
    \;\middle|\;
    \sum_{j=0}^{d-1} b_j = 4n
  \right\},
\]
where each $b_j$ is a nonneg multiple of $\tfrac{1}{m}$.
\end{definition}

Equivalently, writing $c_j = m \cdot b_j$, the lattice consists of
nonneg integer vectors $c = (c_0, \ldots, c_{d-1})$ with
$\sum c_j = S$, where $S = 4nm$.  These are the \emph{compositions}
of the integer $S$ into $d$ nonneg parts.

\begin{lemma}[Lattice as an Approximation --- Lemma~2 of CS17]
\label{lem:lattice-approx}
$B_{n,m}$ is a $\tfrac{1}{m}$-net of $A_n$ in $\ell^\infty$-norm.
That is, for every $a \in A_n$, there exists $b \in B_{n,m}$ with
$\|a - b\|_\infty \le \tfrac{1}{m}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Given $a \in A_n$, round each coordinate to the nearest multiple of
$\tfrac{1}{m}$, adjusting to maintain the sum constraint.
Specifically, use a greedy rounding: process coordinates left to
right, maintaining a running deficit $t_i = \sum_{j<i}(b_j - a_j)$.
Round down first, then alternate to keep $-\tfrac{1}{m} < t_{i+1} \le
0$.  The last coordinate is set to enforce the exact sum.  Each
coordinate satisfies $|a_j - b_j| \le \tfrac{1}{m}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Discretization Error Bound}

Since we approximate $a$ by $b$ with per-coordinate error at most
$\tfrac{1}{m}$, the windowed test value changes by a bounded amount.

\begin{lemma}[Discretization Error --- Lemma~3 of CS17]
\label{lem:disc-error}
For any $a \in A_n$ and its nearest lattice point $b \in B_{n,m}$:
\[
  \tv(a) \;\ge\; \tv(b) - \frac{2}{m} - \frac{1}{m^2},
\]
where $\tv(\cdot)$ denotes the maximum windowed test value from
Lemma~\ref{lem:bin-reduction}.  Equivalently,
\[
  C_{1a} \;\ge\; b_{n,m} - \frac{2}{m} - \frac{1}{m^2},
\]
where $b_{n,m} = \min_{b \in B_{n,m}} \tv(b)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write $a = b + \varepsilon$ where $\|\varepsilon\|_\infty \le
\tfrac{1}{m}$.  Then
\[
  f * f \;=\; g * g + g * \varepsilon + \varepsilon * g
    + \varepsilon * \varepsilon,
\]
where $g$ is the step function with bin averages $b$ and $\varepsilon
= f - g$.  Taking $L^\infty$ norms:
\begin{align*}
  \|f * f\|_\infty
  &\ge \|g * g\|_\infty - 2\|g * \varepsilon\|_\infty
    - \|\varepsilon * \varepsilon\|_\infty.
\end{align*}
Since $g$ is nonneg with $\int g = 1$ and $\|\varepsilon\|_\infty \le
\tfrac{1}{m}$ with support in $(-\tfrac14, \tfrac14)$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\|g * \varepsilon\|_\infty \le \|g\|_1 \cdot
    \|\varepsilon\|_\infty \le 1 \cdot \tfrac{1}{m} = \tfrac{1}{m}$.
    (Young's inequality.)
  \item $\|\varepsilon * \varepsilon\|_\infty \le
    \|\varepsilon\|_1 \cdot \|\varepsilon\|_\infty \le
    \tfrac{1}{m^2}$.
    (Since $\|\varepsilon\|_1 \le d \cdot \tfrac{1}{4n} \cdot
    \tfrac{1}{m} = \tfrac{1}{m}$, because $d = 2n$ and each bin has
    width $\tfrac{1}{4n}$.)
\end{itemize}
Therefore $\|f*f\|_\infty \ge \|g*g\|_\infty - \tfrac{2}{m} -
\tfrac{1}{m^2}$, and since $\|g*g\|_\infty \ge \tv(b)$ by
Lemma~\ref{lem:bin-reduction}, the result follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The correction term in practice]
The correction $\tfrac{2}{m} + \tfrac{1}{m^2}$ is the ``price of
discretization.''  At $m = 20$, it equals $0.1025$; at $m = 50$, it
equals $0.0404$.  Larger $m$ gives a tighter bound but more lattice
points to check.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Refined (Dynamic) Correction}

Lemma~\ref{lem:disc-error} uses a uniform bound on $\|g *
\varepsilon\|_\infty$.  A tighter, position-dependent bound exploits
the support constraint.

\begin{lemma}[Dynamic Correction --- Equation~(1) of CS17]
\label{lem:dynamic-correction}
For a specific window at position $k$ with length $\ell$, let
$W = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}(k,\ell)} b_i$ be the total mass in the
bins that contribute to this window, where $\mathcal{B}(k,\ell) =
\{i : \exists\, j \text{ with } k \le i+j \le k+\ell-2\}$.  Then:
\[
  \tv_{\ell,k}(a) \;\ge\; \tv_{\ell,k}(b) - \frac{2W}{m^2 \cdot 4n\ell}
    - \frac{1}{m^2}.
\]
In integer coordinates (where $c_i = m \cdot b_i$ and
$W_{\text{int}} = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}} c_i$), the
\emph{window-specific effective threshold} is:
\[
  T_{\ell,k} \;=\; c_{\mathrm{target}} + \frac{1 + 2 W_{\text{int}}}{m^2}
\]
rather than the uniform $T = c_{\mathrm{target}} + \tfrac{2}{m} + \tfrac{1}{m^2}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The cross-term $\|g * \varepsilon\|_\infty$ at a specific window
position $k$ is bounded by $\tfrac{1}{m}$ times the integral of $g$
over only those bins that contribute to that window, divided by the
window length.  This gives
$\tfrac{W}{m \cdot 4n\ell}$ for each of the two cross-terms
$g * \varepsilon$ and $\varepsilon * g$, totaling
$\tfrac{2W}{m \cdot 4n\ell}$.

In integer coordinates, the windowed sum
$\sum_{k \le i+j \le k+\ell-2} c_i c_j$ is compared against the
integer threshold:
\[
  \text{int\_thresh}
  \;=\;
  \bigl\lfloor
    \bigl(c_{\mathrm{target}} \cdot m^2 + 1 + 2\,W_{\text{int}}\bigr)
    \cdot \frac{\ell}{4n}
    \cdot (1 - 4\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})
  \bigr\rfloor,
\]
where the $(1 - 4\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$ factor guards against
floating-point rounding, making the threshold conservative (never
over-prunes).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why dynamic is better]
The uniform correction uses $W_{\text{int}} = m$ (the total mass).
The dynamic correction uses the actual mass in contributing bins,
which can be much smaller for narrow windows near the edges of the
convolution domain.  This makes the effective threshold lower
(easier to exceed), allowing more configurations to be pruned.
\end{remark}


% ======================================================================
\section{The Windowed Test Value}\label{sec:testvalue}
% ======================================================================

We now formalize the quantity that the algorithm computes for each
lattice point.

\subsection{Definition}

\begin{definition}
For a mass vector $a = (a_0, \ldots, a_{d-1})$ with $a_i \ge 0$ and
$\sum a_i = 4n$, the \emph{autoconvolution} is the vector of length
$2d - 1$:
\[
  \conv[s] \;=\; \sum_{\substack{i + j = s \\[1pt] 0 \le i,j \le d-1}}
  a_i\, a_j,
  \qquad s = 0, 1, \ldots, 2(d-1).
\]
The \emph{test value} is
\[
  \tv(a) \;=\;
  \max_{\substack{2 \le \ell \le 2d \\[2pt] 0 \le k \le 2(d-1) - (\ell-2)}}
  \frac{1}{4n\ell}
  \sum_{s=k}^{k+\ell-2} \conv[s].
\]
\end{definition}

In words: compute the autoconvolution, then slide a window of each
possible width $\ell$ across it, sum the $\ell - 1$ consecutive
entries in each window position, normalize by $4n\ell$, and take the
maximum.

\subsection{Efficient Computation via Prefix Sums}

The test value can be computed efficiently using prefix sums.  Let
$P[s] = \sum_{k=0}^{s} \conv[k]$ be the prefix sum of the
autoconvolution.  Then the windowed sum from $s_{\text{lo}}$ to
$s_{\text{hi}}$ is:
\[
  \sum_{s=s_\text{lo}}^{s_\text{hi}} \conv[s]
  \;=\;
  P[s_\text{hi}] - P[s_\text{lo} - 1]
  \qquad (\text{with } P[-1] = 0).
\]

\begin{remark}[Complexity]
Computing the autoconvolution takes $O(d^2)$ time (or $O(d \log d)$
via FFT, though the direct method is faster for the small $d$ values
used here).  The prefix sum takes $O(d)$.  Evaluating all windows
takes $O(d^2)$ time (summing over $O(d)$ window widths and $O(d)$
positions each).  Total: $O(d^2)$ per lattice point.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Reversal Symmetry}\label{subsec:reversal}

\begin{theorem}[Autoconvolution Reversal Symmetry]
\label{thm:reversal}
For any mass vector $a$, $\tv(a) = \tv(\rev(a))$, where
$\rev(a) = (a_{d-1}, \ldots, a_0)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof proceeds in three steps.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 1: Autoconvolution reversal.}
Let $a' = \rev(a)$, so $a'_i = a_{d-1-i}$.  Then:
\[
  \conv'[s]
  \;=\; \sum_{i+j=s} a'_i a'_j
  \;=\; \sum_{i+j=s} a_{d-1-i}\, a_{d-1-j}.
\]
Substituting $i' = d-1-i$, $j' = d-1-j$ (so $i'+j' = 2(d-1) - s$):
\[
  \conv'[s] \;=\; \conv[2(d-1) - s].
\]
That is, the autoconvolution of the reversal is the reversal of the
autoconvolution.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 2: Window-sum bijection.}
A window sum of width $w = \ell - 1$ starting at $s_0$ transforms
under reversal to a window sum of the same width starting at
$2(d-1) - s_0 - w + 1$:
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{s=s_0}^{s_0+w-1} \conv'[s]
  &= \sum_{s=s_0}^{s_0+w-1} \conv[2(d-1)-s] \\
  &= \sum_{s'=2(d-1)-s_0-w+1}^{2(d-1)-s_0} \conv[s'].
\end{align*}
This is a window of the same width $w$, so the multiset of all window
sums at width $w$ is identical for $\conv$ and $\conv'$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 3: Normalization preserves the maximum.}
The normalization $\tfrac{1}{4n\ell}$ depends only on $\ell$, not the
window position.  Since the multiset of window sums is identical at
each width, $\tv(a) = \tv(a')$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Canonical Symmetry]
It suffices to check only \emph{canonical} compositions $b$ satisfying
$b \le \rev(b)$ lexicographically.  This halves the search space (up
to palindromes).
\end{corollary}


% ======================================================================
\section{Pruning Strategies}\label{sec:pruning}
% ======================================================================

The lattice $B_{n,m}$ has $\binom{S + d - 1}{d - 1}$ points, which
grows rapidly.  Pruning eliminates configurations without computing
the full test value.

\subsection{Asymmetry Pruning}

\begin{theorem}[Asymmetry Bound]
\label{thm:asymmetry}
Let $f \ge 0$ with $\supp(f) \subseteq (-\tfrac14, \tfrac14)$ and
$\int f = 1$.  Let $L = \int_{-1/4}^{0} f$ be the left-half mass.
Then:
\[
  \|f * f\|_{L^\infty} \;\ge\; 2L^2.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Consider the left-left convolution:
\[
  (f * f)(t) \;=\; \int f(x)\, f(t-x)\, dx
  \;\ge\; \int_{-1/4}^{0} f(x)\, f(t-x)\, \mathbf{1}_{[-1/4,0]}(t-x)\, dx
\]
for $t \in (-\tfrac12, 0)$.  By Fubini's theorem, the total mass of
$f * f$ restricted to $(-\tfrac12, 0)$ is at least $L^2$ (the
left-left contribution).  Since $|(-\tfrac12, 0)| = \tfrac12$:
\[
  \|f * f\|_{L^\infty} \;\ge\; \frac{L^2}{1/2} \;=\; 2L^2.
\]
By symmetry, the same holds for the right-half mass $R = 1 - L$:
$\|f*f\|_\infty \ge 2R^2$.  Therefore
$\|f*f\|_\infty \ge 2 \max(L^2, R^2)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Asymmetry Threshold]
\label{cor:asym-threshold}
If $2L^2 \ge c_{\mathrm{target}}$, i.e.,
$L \ge \sqrt{c_{\mathrm{target}}/2}$, then the configuration is
ruled out.  The threshold left-mass fraction is:
\[
  p^* \;=\; \sqrt{c_{\mathrm{target}} / 2}.
\]
For $c_{\mathrm{target}} = 1.4$: $p^* \approx 0.8367$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Discrete margin]
In the discrete setting, the left-mass fraction
$L_{\text{disc}} = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} c_i / S$ may differ from the
continuous $L$ by up to $\tfrac{1}{4m}$ (due to the rounding
procedure in Lemma~\ref{lem:lattice-approx}).  To be safe, we add
this margin to the threshold:
\[
  p^*_{\text{safe}} \;=\; \sqrt{c_{\mathrm{target}} / 2}
    + \frac{1}{4m}.
\]
A configuration is kept (not pruned) only if
$1 - p^*_{\text{safe}} < L_{\text{disc}} < p^*_{\text{safe}}$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Important subtlety]
The asymmetry bound is a \emph{direct} $L^\infty$ bound on $f * f$.
It does \emph{not} go through the discretization framework of
Lemma~\ref{lem:disc-error}, so we compare against
$c_{\mathrm{target}}$ itself (not $c_{\mathrm{target}} + \tfrac{2}{m}
+ \tfrac{1}{m^2}$).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Contiguous Block-Sum Bound}

\begin{theorem}[Block-Sum Bound]
\label{thm:block-sum}
For any contiguous block of $p$ bins $\{q, q+1, \ldots, q+p-1\}$
with block sum $B = \sum_{i=q}^{q+p-1} c_i$ (in integer coordinates):
\[
  \tv(a) \;\ge\; \frac{B^2}{4n \cdot 2p \cdot m^2}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Consider the window of length $\ell = 2p$ starting at $k = 2q$ (in
sum-index space).  This window captures $\ell - 1 = 2p - 1$
consecutive autoconvolution values at indices $2q, 2q+1, \ldots,
2(q+p-1)$.

All \emph{intra-block} pairs $(i,j)$ with $q \le i,j \le q+p-1$
satisfy $2q \le i+j \le 2(q+p-1)$, so they fall within this window.
Their contribution is:
\[
  \sum_{\substack{q \le i \le q+p-1 \\ q \le j \le q+p-1}} a_i a_j
  \;=\; \left(\sum_{i=q}^{q+p-1} a_i\right)^{\!2}
  \;=\; \frac{B^2}{m^2}.
\]
Since all $a_i \ge 0$, the cross-block pairs contribute $\ge 0$, so
the full window sum is at least $B^2/m^2$.  Normalizing:
\[
  \tv(a) \;\ge\; \frac{B^2/m^2}{4n \cdot 2p}
  \;=\; \frac{B^2}{4n \cdot 2p \cdot m^2}. \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Special cases]
\leavevmode
\begin{itemize}
  \item $p = 1$ (single bin): bound $= c_i^2 / (8n m^2)$.  This is
    the ``max-element'' check.
  \item $p = n$ (half the bins): bound $=
    (\text{left sum})^2 / (4n \cdot d \cdot m^2)$.  This connects to
    the asymmetry argument.
  \item Intermediate $p$ values catch configurations where mass is
    concentrated in a middle block, evading both the max-element and
    asymmetry checks.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Two-Max Cross-Term Bound}

\begin{theorem}[Two-Max Bound]
\label{thm:two-max}
Let $c_{p_1}$ and $c_{p_2}$ be the two largest values in $c$, with
$p_1 \ne p_2$.  Then:
\[
  \tv(a) \;\ge\; \frac{2\, c_{p_1}\, c_{p_2}}{8n\, m^2}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The autoconvolution at index $s = p_1 + p_2$ satisfies:
\[
  \conv[p_1 + p_2]
  \;=\; \sum_{i+j = p_1+p_2} a_i a_j
  \;\ge\; a_{p_1} a_{p_2} + a_{p_2} a_{p_1}
  \;=\; 2\, a_{p_1}\, a_{p_2}
  \;=\; \frac{2\, c_{p_1}\, c_{p_2}}{m^2}.
\]
This single entry is an $\ell = 2$ window:
\[
  \tv(a) \;\ge\; \frac{\conv[p_1+p_2]}{4n \cdot 2}
  \;\ge\; \frac{2\, c_{p_1}\, c_{p_2}}{8n\, m^2}. \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This dominates the single-element bound $c_{p_1}^2 / (8nm^2)$ whenever
$2 c_{p_2} > c_{p_1}$, i.e., when mass is spread between the top two
elements rather than concentrated in one.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Canonical Symmetry Exploitation}

By Theorem~\ref{thm:reversal}, $\tv(b) = \tv(\rev(b))$ for every
lattice point $b$.  We define:

\begin{definition}
A composition $b$ is \emph{canonical} if $b \le \rev(b)$
lexicographically.  That is, comparing $b_0$ with $b_{d-1}$, then
$b_1$ with $b_{d-2}$, etc., $b$ comes first (or they are equal, i.e.,
$b$ is a palindrome).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Canonical Count]
\label{prop:canon-count}
The number of canonical compositions is $\tfrac{N + P}{2}$, where
$N = \binom{S+d-1}{d-1}$ is the total count and $P$ is the number of
palindromes.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Partition all compositions into three classes:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Palindromes ($P$ total): $b = \rev(b)$.  Each is canonical.
  \item Strictly canonical non-palindromes ($C$ total):
    $b < \rev(b)$.
  \item Strictly non-canonical ($C$ total): $b > \rev(b)$.
\end{enumerate}
Classes 2 and 3 are in bijection via reversal, so $N = P + 2C$, hence
$C = (N-P)/2$.  The canonical count is $P + C = (N+P)/2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Loop-bound tightening for $d = 4$]
For $b = (c_0, c_1, c_2, c_3)$ with $c_3 = S - c_0 - c_1 - c_2$,
canonicality ($b \le \rev(b)$) requires $c_0 \le c_3$, giving three
loop bounds:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $c_0 \le \lfloor S/2 \rfloor$ \quad (if $c_0 > S/2$ then
    $c_3 < c_0$).
  \item $c_1 \le S - 2c_0$ \quad (need $c_2 \ge 0$ with
    $c_1 + c_2 \le S - 2c_0$).
  \item $c_2 \le S - 2c_0 - c_1$ \quad (ensures $c_3 \ge c_0$).
\end{itemize}
Plus a palindrome filter: when $c_0 = c_3$, require $c_1 \le c_2$.

For $d = 6$, analogous bounds apply: $c_0 \le \lfloor S/2 \rfloor$,
$c_4 \le S - c_0 - c_1 - c_2 - c_3 - c_0$, with equality checks for
the inner pairs when outer pairs tie.
\end{remark}

\section{The Multiscale Cascade}\label{sec:cascade}
% ======================================================================

Enumerating all compositions at a fine resolution $d$ is
combinatorially explosive.  The cascade strategy starts coarse and
refines only the configurations that survive pruning.

\subsection{Overview}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Step \arabic*:}]
  \item \textbf{Start coarse.}  At level $L_0$, enumerate all
    canonical compositions in $B_{n_0, m}$ with $d_0 = 2n_0$ bins
    (e.g., $n_0 = 2$, $d_0 = 4$).

  \item \textbf{Prune.}  For each composition, apply asymmetry
    pruning and compute the windowed test value.  If the test value
    exceeds the effective threshold $T$, the composition is eliminated.

  \item \textbf{Refine survivors.}  Each surviving composition at
    resolution $d$ is refined to resolution $2d$ by \emph{dyadic
    splitting}: each bin is split into two sub-bins.

  \item \textbf{Repeat.}  Prune the refined compositions.  Continue
    until either all are eliminated (proof complete) or a
    computational budget is reached.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Dyadic Splitting (Refinement)}

Given a parent composition $b = (b_0, \ldots, b_{d-1})$ at resolution
$d$, the refinement produces children $c = (c_0, c_1, \ldots,
c_{2d-1})$ at resolution $2d$, where:
\[
  c_{2i} + c_{2i+1} \;=\; b_i \qquad \text{for each } i = 0, \ldots, d-1,
\]
with $c_{2i}, c_{2i+1} \ge 0$ and both being nonneg integers.

\subsection{Energy Cap}\label{subsec:energy-cap}

A child bin with value $c_j$ contributes $a_j^2 = (c_j/m)^2 \cdot
(4n_{\text{child}})^2$ to the $\ell=2$ diagonal window.  If this
single-bin contribution already exceeds the threshold, the child is
immediately pruned.  Therefore, we cap each child bin at:
\[
  x_{\text{cap}} \;=\;
  \left\lfloor m \cdot \sqrt{\frac{T}{d_{\text{child}}}} \right\rfloor,
\]
where $T = c_{\mathrm{target}} + \tfrac{2}{m} + \tfrac{1}{m^2}$ is the
effective threshold and $d_{\text{child}} = 2d$.  Any child with a bin
exceeding $x_{\text{cap}}$ would be pruned by the $\ell=2$ window, so
we never generate it.

\begin{proposition}[Energy Cap Soundness]
If $c_j > x_{\mathrm{cap}}$, then
$a_j^2 / (4 n_{\mathrm{child}} \cdot 2) > T$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We have $a_j = c_j \cdot 4n_{\text{child}} / m$ and
$d_{\text{child}} = 2n_{\text{child}}$.  Then:
\[
  \frac{a_j^2}{4 n_{\mathrm{child}} \cdot 2}
  = \frac{c_j^2 \cdot 2 n_{\mathrm{child}}}{m^2}.
\]
With $c_j > m\sqrt{T/(2n_{\mathrm{child}})}$:
\[
  \frac{c_j^2 \cdot 2 n_{\mathrm{child}}}{m^2}
  > \frac{m^2 \cdot T/(2n_{\mathrm{child}}) \cdot 2n_{\mathrm{child}}}{m^2}
  = T. \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Cascade Soundness}

\begin{theorem}[Cascade Soundness]
\label{thm:cascade-sound}
If the cascade terminates with zero survivors at some level $L_k$
(with resolution $d_k = d_0 \cdot 2^k$), then
$C_{1a} \ge c_{\mathrm{target}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We show by induction that every composition in $B_{n_k, m}$ (the
lattice at level $k$) is either:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
  \item directly pruned at level $k$ (its test value exceeds $T$), or
  \item an ancestor of some composition at level $k+1$ that is itself
    pruned.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Base case ($L_0$):}  At level $L_0$, all canonical
compositions of $B_{n_0, m}$ are enumerated.  By reversal symmetry
(Theorem~\ref{thm:reversal}), the non-canonical compositions have
identical test values to their canonical counterparts, so checking
canonicals suffices.

\textbf{Inductive step:}  A level-$k$ composition $b$ that is
\emph{not} directly pruned survives to be refined.  Its children
(level-$k+1$ compositions) are \emph{all} generated and tested.
If every child is pruned, then $b$ is indirectly eliminated:
any continuous function $f$ whose bin averages at resolution $d_k$
are close to $b$ must have bin averages at resolution $d_{k+1}$
that are close to one of $b$'s children, and that child is pruned.

\textbf{Termination:}  If level $L_k$ has zero survivors, then every
composition at resolution $d_k$ is pruned (directly or ancestrally).
Since $B_{n_k, m}$ is a $\tfrac{1}{m}$-net of $A_{n_k}$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:lattice-approx}), every continuous vector $a$ has a
nearby lattice point whose test value exceeds $T$.  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:disc-error}:
\[
  \tv(a) \;\ge\; \tv(b) - \frac{2}{m} - \frac{1}{m^2}
  \;\ge\; T - \frac{2}{m} - \frac{1}{m^2}
  \;=\; c_{\mathrm{target}}.
\]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:bin-reduction}, $\|f*f\|_\infty \ge \tv(a) \ge
c_{\mathrm{target}}$ for every $f$.  Therefore $C_{1a} \ge
c_{\mathrm{target}}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Canonical Handling Across Levels}

At level $L_0$, only canonical compositions are generated.  At levels
$L_1$ and beyond, the situation is more subtle:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Children of a canonical parent $P$ are \emph{not} necessarily
    canonical.
  \item The reversal $\rev(P)$ is \emph{not} in the parent list
    (unless $P$ is a palindrome), so its children are not generated
    directly.
  \item However, if $C$ is a child of $P$, then $\rev(C)$ is a child
    of $\rev(P)$.  Since $\tv(C) = \tv(\rev(C))$
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:reversal}), testing $C$ suffices for both.
\end{itemize}

Therefore at levels $\ge 1$:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Generate \emph{all} children of each parent (no canonical
    filter during generation).
  \item Test each child against the threshold.
  \item \emph{Canonicalize} survivors: replace each with
    $\min(C, \rev(C))$ lexicographically.
  \item \emph{Deduplicate}: remove duplicate canonical forms (children
    from different parents, or a palindrome parent producing both $C$
    and $\rev(C)$).
\end{enumerate}

\begin{proposition}[Completeness of Canonical Handling]
This procedure tests every composition at the child resolution:
if $C'$ is any child-level composition and $P' = \text{parent}(C')$,
then either $P'$ or $\rev(P')$ is in the parent list, and $C'$ or
$\rev(C')$ is among the tested children.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Fused Generate-and-Prune Kernel}

At deep cascade levels, each parent may have millions of children.
The fused kernel generates and tests each child inline via a
Cartesian-product odometer, never materializing the full children
array:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Incremental autoconvolution:} when only the last
    cursor position changes ($\sim$67\% of steps), the raw
    autoconvolution is updated in $O(d)$ instead of $O(d^2)$.
  \item \textbf{Quick-check:} re-tries the previous killing window
    before a full scan.
  \item \textbf{Subtree pruning:} on deep carries, checks the
    partial autoconvolution of fixed bins against worst-case unfixed
    mass; if it exceeds the threshold, the entire subtree is skipped.
\end{itemize}

\section{The Complete Proof}\label{sec:algorithm}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Prove $C_{1a} \ge c_{\text{target}}$}
\label{alg:cascade}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $c_{\text{target}}$, grid resolution $m$, starting half-bins
  $n_0$, max levels $L_{\max}$
\State $T \gets c_{\text{target}} + 2/m + 1/m^2$
  \Comment{Effective threshold}
\State $d_0 \gets 2 n_0$
\State Generate all canonical compositions of $S = 4 n_0 m$ into
  $d_0$ parts
\State Apply asymmetry pruning (threshold
  $\sqrt{c_{\text{target}}/2} + 1/(4m)$)
\State Compute test values for remaining compositions; prune those
  with $\tv \ge T$
\State $\text{survivors}_0 \gets$ unpruned compositions
\For{$k = 1, 2, \ldots, L_{\max}$}
  \State $d_k \gets 2 d_{k-1}$; \quad
    $n_k \gets 2 n_{k-1}$
  \State $x_{\text{cap}} \gets \lfloor m \sqrt{T / d_k} \rfloor$
  \ForAll{parent $P \in \text{survivors}_{k-1}$}
    \ForAll{children $C$ of $P$ (dyadic split with energy cap)}
      \State Apply asymmetry pruning to $C$
      \If{not asymmetry-pruned}
        \State Compute $\tv(C)$ via windowed autoconvolution
        \If{$\tv(C) \ge T$ (using dynamic per-window threshold)}
          \State Prune $C$
        \Else
          \State $C$ survives; canonicalize and store
        \EndIf
      \EndIf
    \EndFor
  \EndFor
  \State Deduplicate survivors (canonicalize $\to$ sort $\to$ scan)
  \State $\text{survivors}_k \gets$ deduplicated survivors
  \If{$|\text{survivors}_k| = 0$}
    \State \Return \textsc{Proven}: $C_{1a} \ge c_{\text{target}}$
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return \textsc{Inconclusive}:
  $|\text{survivors}_{L_{\max}}|$ compositions remain
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Main Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Main Result]
\label{thm:main}
If Algorithm~\ref{alg:cascade} returns \textsc{Proven} with
parameters $(c_{\mathrm{target}}, m, n_0)$, then
$C_{1a} \ge c_{\mathrm{target}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof follows from the chain of lemmas:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item By Lemma~\ref{lem:bin-reduction}, for any $f \in \mathcal{F}$
    with $\int f = 1$ and corresponding bin averages $a$ at resolution
    $d_k$:
    $\|f*f\|_\infty \ge \tv(a)$.

  \item By Lemma~\ref{lem:lattice-approx}, there exists a lattice
    point $b \in B_{n_k, m}$ with $\|a - b\|_\infty \le 1/m$.

  \item By Lemma~\ref{lem:disc-error}:
    $\tv(a) \ge \tv(b) - \tfrac{2}{m} - \tfrac{1}{m^2}$.

  \item By Theorem~\ref{thm:cascade-sound}, since the cascade
    terminated with zero survivors, every $b \in B_{n_k, m}$ satisfies
    $\tv(b) \ge T = c_{\mathrm{target}} + \tfrac{2}{m} + \tfrac{1}{m^2}$.

  \item Combining:
    \[
      \|f*f\|_\infty
      \;\ge\; \tv(a)
      \;\ge\; \tv(b) - \frac{2}{m} - \frac{1}{m^2}
      \;\ge\; T - \frac{2}{m} - \frac{1}{m^2}
      \;=\; c_{\mathrm{target}}.
    \]

  \item Since this holds for every $f \in \mathcal{F}$ with
    $\int f = 1$, by definition $C_{1a} \ge c_{\mathrm{target}}$.
    \qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

\section{Correctness Invariants}\label{sec:correctness}
% ======================================================================

For the proof to be mathematically valid, the implementation must
satisfy four invariants.

\subsection{Invariant 1: Complete Enumeration}

Every composition $b \in B_{n,m}$ must be either directly checked or
shown to be covered by a checked ancestor and all its refinements.

\begin{itemize}
  \item At $L_0$: all $\binom{S + d_0 - 1}{d_0 - 1}$ compositions
    are enumerated (in canonical form, with reversal symmetry).
    Verified by comparing the generator output count against the
    stars-and-bars formula, and by checking completeness, uniqueness,
    non-negativity, and sum constraints.
  \item At $L_k$ ($k \ge 1$): all children of all surviving parents
    are generated (no sampling or probabilistic skipping).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Invariant 2: All Windows Checked}

For each composition that is not eliminated by asymmetry, every valid
window $(\ell, k)$ with $2 \le \ell \le 2d$ and all valid starting
positions $k$ must be tested before the composition is declared a
survivor.

\subsection{Invariant 3: Conservative Arithmetic}

Floating-point errors must not cause false positives (wrongly ruling
out a valid configuration whose true test value is below $T$).

\begin{itemize}
  \item Integer coordinates: the autoconvolution is computed in
    integer arithmetic ($c_i c_j$ are exact integers), avoiding
    round-off entirely.
  \item Conservative threshold: the integer threshold includes a
    $(1 - 4\epsilon_{\text{mach}})$ safety factor, making it
    \emph{lower} than the true mathematical threshold.  This means
    the check $\text{ws} > \text{int\_thresh}$ is a \emph{sufficient}
    condition for pruning, never a false positive.
  \item The additional $+1 + 10^{-9} m^2$ term in the base threshold
    further ensures conservatism.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Invariant 4: Refinement Completeness}

Every refinement of a surviving parent must be checked at the finer
level.  The dyadic splitting with energy cap generates all valid
children (Section~\ref{subsec:energy-cap}), and children exceeding the
energy cap are provably pruned.

The correctness of the implementation has been verified through
multiple independent checks.

\subsection{Composition Generators}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Completeness}: For 7 parameter combinations $(d, S)$,
    the generator output count matches $\binom{S+d-1}{d-1}$ exactly.
    All outputs sum to $S$, are non-negative, and are unique.
  \item \textbf{Specialized $\equiv$ generic}: The $d=4$ and $d=6$
    specialized generators produce identical sets to the generic
    stack-based generator.
  \item \textbf{Canonical correctness}: For 8 parameter combinations,
    canonical generators produce exactly $\tfrac{N+P}{2}$ outputs, all
    satisfying $b \le \rev(b)$, with every non-canonical $b$ having
    $\rev(b)$ in the output.
  \item \textbf{Batch boundaries}: Across 9 configurations and 8
    batch sizes, output is identical regardless of where batch
    boundaries fall (160 checks, all pass).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Test Value Computation}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Reversal symmetry ($\tv(b) = \tv(\rev(b))$) verified
    exhaustively for $d=4, S=8$ (165 compositions) and $d=6, S=6$
    (462 compositions), plus 1,500 random tests.  Zero mismatches.
  \item Pure-Python reference implementation agrees with Numba JIT
    kernel on all 891 canonical compositions at $d=4, m=20$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Pruning Correctness}

\begin{itemize}
  \item No over-pruning: zero compositions pruned whose true test
    value is below the dynamic threshold.
  \item No missed prunes: zero compositions not pruned whose true
    test value exceeds the dynamic threshold.
  \item Asymmetry filter verified to be exactly symmetric under
    reversal.
\end{itemize}

The cascade prover's dynamic threshold formula has been verified

Cloninger--Steinerberger, with the Python implementation being
strictly more conservative (harder to prune) due to safety margins.


% ======================================================================
\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Relationship to the Original CS17 Proof}

Our framework is a direct extension of Cloninger \& Steinerberger
(2017).  The key differences:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Dynamic correction}: We use the per-window
    position-dependent correction (Lemma~\ref{lem:dynamic-correction})
    instead of the uniform $\tfrac{2}{m} + \tfrac{1}{m^2}$.  This
    prunes more aggressively while remaining mathematically rigorous.

  \item \textbf{Multiscale cascade}: CS17 used $m = 50$ and refined
    from $n = 3$ through $n = 24$.  We use $m = 20$ with dyadic
    refinement from $d = 4$ through $d = 64$, exploiting the fused
    kernel's efficiency.

  \item \textbf{Fused generation}: Instead of materializing all
    children and then testing, we generate and test each child inline,
    reducing memory from $O(N_{\text{children}} \cdot d)$ to $O(d)$
    per parent.

  \item \textbf{Integer arithmetic}: Autoconvolution and threshold
    comparisons use exact integer operations, with floating-point
    involved only in the conservative threshold computation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item The proof is \emph{computer-assisted}: the mathematical
    argument reduces correctness to a finite computation, but that
    computation must be performed by a machine.  The invariants in
    Section~\ref{sec:correctness} ensure that the computation is
    trustworthy.

  \item The approach is limited by the exponential growth of the
    composition count with $d$ and $m$.  The multiscale cascade
    mitigates this, but very aggressive targets
    ($c_{\mathrm{target}}$ close to $C_{1a}$) will require more
    cascade levels and higher computational cost.
\end{itemize}
